Three Countries, One Throughline

I was born in Korea, raised and educated in New Zealand, and built my career across three distinct professional cultures before landing at Apple. That path is unusual — most "bilingual" professionals know two cultures well. I've operated inside three: New Zealand's small-business economy, Korea's media and language market, and a decade inside one of the most demanding customer-facing operations in U.S. tech.

Each move added a layer instead of replacing the last one. Managing a 16-person contact-center team in Auckland taught me how to run operations at scale under real KPI pressure. A short stint in Korean film production taught me how creative and commercial priorities get negotiated in the same room. Nine years reviewing apps at Apple taught me how a global platform actually keeps hundreds of millions of experiences safe, consistent, and shippable — one submission at a time.

That path also made me the translator in the room — native Korean, full professional English, and years as the working bridge between headquarters and international teams: across Apple's global review organization, and before that between a New Zealand telecom's East Asian customer base and its head office. Not just linguistically — operationally.

What I Actually Do

Day to day, I sit with teams that are trying to move a customer-experience idea or a brand-new product category from "we think this could work" to "here's something you can click through." I use AI-native tooling to build interactive, single-file prototypes — real enough to react to, fast enough to build inside the same working session the idea came up in. That skill sits on top of twenty years of hands-on operations: seven years running a contact center at Spark New Zealand, and ten years inside Apple's App Store watching how apps actually launch, break, get flagged, and get fixed.

Why Prototyping, Why Now

Nine years of first-touch exposure to nearly every category to pass through the App Store — cryptocurrency and Web3, generative AI, subscription and live-service products, and spatial computing from visionOS's very first submissions — gave me an unusually broad view of how emerging categories actually behave once real users and real regulators show up. AI-native tooling (I build with Claude CLI) finally lets me turn that pattern recognition into something concrete, fast: a working prototype a team can react to in the same meeting the idea came up in, instead of a spec that gets debated for a month. How this was made

Under 12 Hours, No Traditional Code

I'm not a developer by trade — I'm a media and video production professional, which means everything audiovisual is native territory: composition, pacing, motion, sound, and how people actually experience a screen. That literacy is what I direct AI with.

Everything here — this résumé app and every prototype linked above — took under 12 hours of total working time, orchestrating Claude alongside other AI tools for images, audio, and 3D assets. The craft isn't typing code; it's knowing exactly what to ask for, judging what comes back with a producer's eye, and iterating until it feels right.

Where this goes next

Beyond the Screen — AI as an Operating Skill

Prototyping is the visible half of how I work with AI. The same directing skill applies to the invisible half: deep, structured analysis of documents and images; local on-device models (Ollama and similar) where privacy or cost demands it; and turning broad, messy information into business-ready strategy. I haven't had a production stage for that side of the work yet — which is exactly the kind of stage I'm looking for. What the projects above prove is the transferable part: I can design the method, judge the output, and iterate until it actually solves the problem.
